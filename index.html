Project Group 09: SeaCONEX Visualizing Maritime Supply Chain Networks, CS 7250 S21

Mason Leon, Rithika Lakshminarayanan, Alice Tilles

Service-Learning Course Project as part of CS 7250 S21 Information Visualization: Theory and Applications , taught by Prof. Cody Dunne , Data Visualization @ Khoury , Northeastern University .

Abstract

The Container Express also known as the CONEX, was introduced by the United States Military during the Korean War as an intermodal logistics platform to improve cargo flows and operations throughout supply chain networks. The CONEX and Malcolm McLean's intermodal container were simple freight technology concepts that revolutionized supply chains. Containerization and the equipment utilized to transport goods efficiently have fostered globalization and made the modern world. However, supply chains remain vulnerable to disruption and are plagued by poor visibility and difficulty exchanging information about cargo and freight networks. Supply chain disruption and the complexity of maritime and hinterland networks remain key issues around the world. SeaCONEX is not just a portmanteau, but a visualization tool focused on this larger issue by allowing users to see the connections of maritime supply chain networks. In this paper, we present a visualization dashboard that allows users to evaluate carrier service rotation between terminals, view the shortest navigable marine route between connections, and investigate vessel trajectories and deviations from the optimal route.

Visualization

The visualizations below depicts a hypothetical scenario where the UKMOD is trying to transport a shipment from London to the UK Overseas Territory of Bermuda. In this case study, UKMOD needs to evaluate commercial shipping options between the UK and Bermuda across 6 ocean carriers, 9 services, 18 deployed vessels, and their network of ports. The vessels include several Container/Roll-On Roll-Off (ConRo), geared container vessels, and fully-cellular container vessels actively deployed on the Trans-Atlantic and US-Bermuda trade lanes.

To support the two important tasks of 1) examining connectivity between ports with seemingly disjoint carrier networks and 2) tracking vessel trajectories based on AIS data; we present two views that allow users to accomplish these tasks. The first view shows connections between ports in a node-link diagram and allows users to filter routes based on carriers. The second view allows users to examine actual paths taken by vessels and their deviation from the shortest or most ideal routes in red.

Visualization explanation

For the node-link diagram, we chose a very simple rendering. Marine terminals are in blue and, and when selected, change color to red, taking advantage of the pop-out effect which brings attention to terminals of interest. On hovering over a marine terminal, users can see more details like the name of the marine terminal and the carriers that operate their vessels there. The advantage of the node-link diagram is that it removes geographic interference, allowing the user to focus clearly on which ports are connected, without worrying about the distance between them, ocean conditions between them, etc. This can help remove bias on "best" or "most connected" ports, and just examine the data.
