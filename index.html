Project Group 09: SeaCONEX Visualizing Maritime Supply Chain Networks, CS 7250 S21

Mason Leon, Rithika Lakshminarayanan, Alice Tilles

Service-Learning Course Project as part of CS 7250 S21 Information Visualization: Theory and Applications , taught by Prof. Cody Dunne , Data Visualization @ Khoury , Northeastern University .

Abstract

Supply chain operations and the world of transportation are often hidden from the general public, that is until disaster strikes or an eagerly awaited online purchase is delayed. The complexity of the global supply chain can make it unfathomable to track when "things go wrong", items are "lost in the mail", or how an entire truck trailer could "vanish". For professional supply chain managers and logisticians, even with the availability of abundant internal and external data sets, it can be difficult to communicate the right information to stakeholders or to investigate data and determine broken links in the supply chain.

For organizations responsible for the defense and security of a nation's citizens, economy, and strategic interests, a firm understanding of logistics and transportation operations is essential. This project seeks to develop new innovative visualization approaches to improve the presentation of logistics data and tools to examine chokepoints in the maritime supply chain for the United States Department of Transportation Volpe National Transportation Systems Center (Volpe) and the United Kingdom Ministry of Defense (UKMOD) .

The proposed supply chain visualization approach focuses on a case study developed for Volpe and the UKMOD support a hypothetical shipment from London to the UK Overseas Territory of Bermuda utilizing commercial maritime carriers.

Visualization

The visualization below depicts the network for Independent Container Line (ICL). ICL is a niche ocean carrier with a single Eastbound and Westbound TAC1 service in the Trans-Atlantic trade. TAC1 links the following marine terminals:

Due to great circle lines, the links currently appear crossed. We plan to utilize the searoute tool of EU EUROSTAT to generate GeoJSON shortest path line segments between the port pairs. Through linking we will hide labels for ports out of network.
